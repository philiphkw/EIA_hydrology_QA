The goal of ACGIH (and other Agencies) is to recommend TLVs (or their equivalent) for all substances for which
There is evidence of health effects at concentrations in workplace air.
To date, no TLV has been established, although this material may cause adverse health effects (as
evidenced in animal experiments or clinical experience). Airborne concentrations should be kept as low as
practically possible and occupational exposure should be kept to a minimum.
Sensory irritants are chemicals that produce temporary and undesirable side effects in the eyes, nose or throat.
Historically, occupational exposure standards for these irritants have been based on observation of workers' responses to
various concentrations in the air. Current expectations require that almost every individual be protected against even the slightest irritation.
Sensory and exposure standards are established using uncertainty or safety factors of 5 to 10 or more. Sometimes
Animal no-observable-effect-levels (NOEL) are used to determine these limits when
results in humans are not available. An additional method, typically used by the TLV (USA) committee in determining
respiratory standards for this group of chemicals, has been to assign limit values (TLV C) to rapidly acting irritants and assign
short-term exposure limits (TLV STELs) when the weight of evidence for irritation, bioaccumulation and other factors are combined to
guarantee such a limit. In contrast to the MAK Commission (Germany) uses a five-category system based on intense odor, local irritation, and
elimination half-life. However this system is being replaced to be consistent with the European Union (EU) Scientific
Committee for Occupational Exposure Limits (SCOEL); this is more closely related to that of the United States. OSHA (U.S.
SA) concluded
that exposure to sensory irritants can cause:
