EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
The following report presents the Analysis of climate change scenarios based on trends in hydrometeorological variables and derived extreme events, carried out for Aclara; for the purpose of the environmental impact evaluation of the Mining Development project for the Extraction of Clays for the Production of Rare Earth Concentrate." To this end, a historical characterization of the hydrometeorology of a study area containing the commune of Penco was carried out, in particular for the Coastal Islands basins between Río Itata and Río Biobío. And projections of trends indicators of extreme events were generated, based on different climate change models and emission scenarios, of future hydrometeorological conditions. that could affect the work.
9 measurement stations were analyzed with a record of 709 0 more observations for the study period, between 1985 and 2019. Of the 9 selected stations, only one measures precipitation (pp) and temperature (Carriel Sur); another seven measure only precipitation (Dichato, Rafael, Cancha los Litres, Andalien; Concepción DGA, Cerro Caracol and Nonguén Essbio) and one measures flow (Rio Andalien on the way to Penco).
The historical analysis indicates that there is a significant trend towards a decrease in precipitation in April (Carriel Sur) and May (Cancha Los Litres), and also on an annual scale for Dichato. The flow also presents a statistical decrease in July and on an annual scale. Likewise, an increase in the minimum temperature in the summer (December to February) is shown. Other indicators; as TDD (days with precipitation less than mm) and Qmax (maximum flows), show a significant decreasing trend. Likewise, indicators such as WarmNights (days when the temperature
minimum temperature exceeds the 90th percentile of the record) and SummerDays (number of days in which the maximum temperature exceeds 25 C) show a significant increasing trend in certain seasons. On the contrary, indicators such as R95 (days with precipitation with percentile greater than 0 equal to 95), MinTmax (minimum temperature of maximum temperatures) present evidence of increase and decrease in some stations.
Based on the guidelines of the Methodological Guide for the Consideration of Climate Change in the SEIA, CMIPS climate projection models were analyzed through the product reported in the ARClim project, in the RCP8.5 projection to analyze the future period between the years 2020 and 2054. 3 models were selected that best represented the study area when comparing the historical data of the projections with the data observed at the measurement stations: The selected models were NorESMI-M

GLOSSARY OF TERMS AND ABBREVIATIONS
AR: For its acronym in English, evaluation report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). Elsexto is the last report published as of 2021 (for the WGI).
Climate change: Variation in the state of the climate according to the change in the value and/or variability of ITS average properties, statistically verified. It is identified by being persistent over time; as decades longer periods.
CMIP: Associated Climate Projection Coupled Model Intercomparison Project.
Bias correction: Method by which the values ​​of a model are corrected by using observations; so that at the average level they have the same mean.
Downscaling: Methodology to reduce the scale of spatial representation of GCM's and attribute them to a context of historical observations in a study area; preserving the original signal of the model.
Ensemble: Set of model simulations that characterize a climate projection based on its initial conditions and forcing.
Radiative forcing: Change in energy flux caused by a driving element and quantified at the top of the atmosphere in WIm?.
Forcing: Corresponds to a series of elements that condition the behavior of a system; given its properties.
GCM: General Circulation Models of the atmosphere. Used to carry out global climate simulations under different emissions scenarios.
GHG: Greenhouse Gases (GHG), atmospheric gases that absorb and emit radiation in the infrared spectrum. The main GHGs are water vapor (H20), carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O) and Ozone (03).

1. INTRODUCTION
Rare earth elements (0 Rare Earth Elements (REEs) are known as a set of 17 specific chemical elements: Scandium (Sc) Yttrium (Y) and 15 elements from the lanthanide group (lanthanum (La) cerium (Ce) praseodymium (Pr)' neodymium (Nd)' promethium (Pm)' samarium (Sm)' europium (Eu)' gadolinium (Gd), terbium (Tb), dysprosium (Dy), holmium (Ho), erbium (Er), thulium TM ytterbium (Yb) and lutetium (Lu)), which together with the actinides (which are not considered rare earths) make up the elements known as 'internal transition' elements.
These rare earths do not necessarily imply that they are elements that are scarce in availability, but rather that it is rare to find them in their pure state, so their mining is usually localized (Mariano, 2018). In that sense, the deposits of these rare earths are mainly associated with 4 types of rocks:
Carbonatites: Igneous rocks with a content greater than 50% of carbonates. Alkaline igneous rocks. Lateritic clays: Which result from in situ alteration of previous sites. Placer type: Surface sedimentary deposits of considerable extent (gold, platinum, tin, detritus).
The use of these rare earths is linked to industrial applications, associated for example with electric motors (samarium), computer information storage (ytterbium terbium), televisions (europium, yttrium) and others such as refrigeration; batteries, microwaves; etc. (Regueiro; 2008).
Clarifies; is a company dedicated to Rare Earth extraction with a development project in Chile, which has developed a sustainable and innovative extraction process; which does not use explosives, crushing or grinding; nor does it produce solid or liquid waste, in addition to recycling 95% of the water it uses. Along with this, the clay that is extracted in the pits is replaced to later reforest the land with native/autochthonous species. In Penco, Biobío region, Aclara develops a project module for the extraction of rare earths in a sustainable way, in a low-scale initiative that will avoid the generation of dust AND noise since it does not require explosives, crushing or grinding, will use treated wastewater, will recirculate water by treating it and will be responsible for reforesting the area with protected endemic species.

2.2. Critical analysis of historical hydrometeorological conditions in the Coastal and Islands basin between the Itata River and the Biobío River (Penco Commune)
An analysis of trend patterns of precipitation, temperature and flow variables was carried out based on observations from nine hisdrometeorological stations belonging to the General Directorate of Water (DGA) for the period 1985-2019 with data availability equal to or greater than 70% in the aforementioned reference period. In this way, of the nine stations that meet that quality in their records, only one measures precipitation (pp) and temperature (Carriel Sur); another seven measure only precipitation (Dichato Rafael, Cancha los Litres, Andalien, Concepción DGA, Cerro Caracol and Nonguén Essbio) and one measures flow (Rio Andalien on the way to Penco). An analysis of trends and extreme events was carried out on these observations by adjusting the slope of the regression curve according to the corresponding type of aggregation (monthly; seasonal or annual), combining it with obtaining the p value from the Mann-Kendall test. To obtain the trends of both the source variables and the indicators derived from them, the daily data series must be complete for the period studied. Thus, those stations selected with a quality equal to or greater than 70% must go through an information filling process that completes the missing records consistently, in order not to lose the temporal signal of the series.
==================================================================================
PAGE 22
==================================================================================
For this filling of climate information, the algorithm developed by the consulting team together with the University of Chile in the context of the national water balance update project (DGA, 2018) was considered. In this, filling is done by estimating cross-correlations between surrounding stations for each month's data. That is, all the days associated with Januarys, Februarys, and the remaining months until December are compared in the historical period respectively. If, from this linear relationship, the resulting determination coefficient (R2) is greater than 0.7, the candidate series is completed by means of a simple linear regression, to then incorporate a stochastic noise factor (8) that adds natural variability to the result but preserves the original signal of the series. this according to the formula:
T1 = a + bT2 + 1 ~R2 . 6. S1
Where T' is the estimate of the variable at the station (candidate) and T2 the concurrent value measured at regressor/filler station 2; in the respective month. a is a single value corresponding to the intercept of the simple linear regression, while b corresponds to the slope of the regression. On the other hand, R2 is the coefficient of determination of the linear relationship, 6 is a random number derived from the standard Normal distribution (u=0.0=1) and S1 is the standard deviation of the records from station 1.
This process is carried out iteratively at the stations to be filled until they converge under three conditions: 1) All records have been completed; 2) A returning station cannot fill the same station more than once and 3) the value of the R2 is lower than the defined threshold (0.7). In this sense, if after this step there are still uncompleted records, the CRZMet data (Boisier et al. 2018) is used to fill in the missing gaps based on the statistical relationship with the observations. Finally, if even with this there are gaps in information; They are completed with the median of the series for that day of the year. With these results, it is now possible to analyze trend patterns in the data.
For this trend analysis; The data are grouped in four ways: on a monthly scale, on an annual scale and on a seasonal scale, obtaining the trends and the p value from the Mann-Kendall test (McLeod, 2005) always for a series of 30 data. For example, to obtain the trend of maximum temperatures in January, the average of the Januarys in the analyzed period is taken and these data are used to obtain the regression slope: The same applies if it were done for the autumns; All values ​​are added per year for each astronomical autumn and the trend signal for these data is obtained. Finally, on an annual scale; information is added by year

To solve this purpose, a climate model selection methodology is used that is based on the association of the data obtained from observations (stations) with those from each model in a coincident historical period (currently 1985-2019). This relationship is measured based on two statisticians: a) The Annual Average Bias (SCA)' calculated as the quotient between the annual average of precipitation 0 temperature of model 1 interpolated at station i, and the same value calculated at station i,

4. CONCLUSIONS
Within the framework of this report, an analysis has been carried out of the study area defined between the DARH sub-basins of the Andalien River and the coastal ones between the Pingueral River and the Andalien River; which are limited to the commune of Penco, and which in turn are part of the Coastal Islands basin between the Itata River and the Biobío River, and which include the areas where the Aclara project works will be located.
After specially characterizing this area also based on other related delimitations; like Camels-CL; and after analyzing the state of the art with respect to what was reported in the context of the present in the area; A hydrometeorological characterization was carried out, which showed as the main results in the period 1985-2019 a sustained and significant decrease on an annual scale in precipitation in the stations in the area and an increase in the minimum temperature that was also significant in the summer months. There are representative decreases in the flows of the Rio Andalien station on the way to Penco on an annual scale and in the month of July, but not in the rest of the months. The above; translate into less precipitation that can affect winter flows, which in turn can be more pressured by greater evapotranspiration demands in the summer months given the increase in temperatures.
For its part, in the analysis of indicators of extreme events there is a general increase in the area of ​​days without rain in the autumn months, which is significant in practically all the stations analyzed, which could influence in the future the increase in conditions of water scarcity reported in some drought indicator. The days in which precipitation exceeds a high threshold (959th percentile) have been decreasing on an annual scale; However, when analyzed based on climate change models, an increase in rainfall events that exceed the record in these months is suggested.
The indicators linked to the increase in maximum temperatures have greater significance in summer and on an annual scale, so increases in reports of heat waves are expected according to the meteorological history of the area. For its part, there is a significant and generalized historical decrease in the maximum flows of the Rio Andalien road Penco station, in the winter and spring months and on an annual scale, so there may be more pressures, for example on various crops.

Based on the selected climate change models, heterogeneous trends are reported in some indicators that do not allow establishing a clear pattern of change. However, where there is agreement is in the potential progressive increase in the days in which it does not rain, on an annual scale AND the increase in extreme precipitation events in summer months. In the case of extreme temperatures, warmer days and nights are generally expected and increased
progressive temperature cough, especially in summer; which can, for example, increase the evapotranspiration demands of crops and increase, due to the potential increase in dry fuel; the risk of forest fires.
In summary, 1) Climatic conditions are changing and are expected to change in the future with the following main characteristics. This must be verified in the Climatic Baseline of the ElA_ 2) These climatic conditions affect the base hydrological conditions in the relevant basins of the project's area of ​​influence. However; The project, by using water from the treatment plant and recirculating a significant percentage, should not be affected. 3) Climatic conditions of lower precipitation and higher temperature indicate a water deficit that must be considered in the design and monitoring of restoration plans.
