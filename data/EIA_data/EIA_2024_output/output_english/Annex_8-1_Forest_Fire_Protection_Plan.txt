1. INTRODUCTION
Forest and/or vegetation fires are one of the main natural disturbances
that affect forests around the world, becoming a great concern due to the
significant increase in its occurrence and severity (Westerling et al. 2006). The changes in the
climate and its variability (Westerling et al. 2006), as well as changes in land use
and the expansion of the urban-forest interface (Keeley et al. 1999), have been indicated as the
main factors that would be contributing to a greater frequency and extension of the
fires.
Particularly in Chile, forest fires are perceived as one of the main
environmental problems (Castillo et al. 2003), attracting significant attention from
media and society, which has been accentuated by the large fires that occurred during the
recent years (González 2005, Urzúa and Cáceres 2011). The Biobío Region is responsible for a
important participation, with 38.9% of the fires and 28.6% of the affected area
(according to values from 10 seasons, 1994 - 2004).
Forest and vegetation fires are caused mainly by the effect of
activities associated with man, therefore, fires as a consequence of natural phenomena
(lightning, volcanic eruptions, etc.) are rare in Chile. The consequences of a fire
forestry range from economic, environmental, cultural impacts and even losses of
human lives.
The Mining Development Project of Clay Extraction for the Production of Concentrate
Rare Earths, corresponds to the set of works and activities that will allow the construction and
operation of a mining site and a clay processing plant, a material present in
the area, which due to natural effects is found in the area due to the alteration of the materials
existing lithologies in the area, with the presence of lanthanides adsorbed in the minerals of
clay.
The project is located in a rural environment, in the Communes of Penco and Concepción, Biobío region.
(Figure 1-1).

The objectives of this program are:
Establish preventive and forest fire fighting measures, identifying sectors
critical or high fire risk, where the probability of fire is greater, depending on the
property and area information. The choice of critical sectors is made considering the
potential human activities inside and outside the project's area of influence, as well as the
type of vegetation and existing topography.
For each critical or high fire risk sector identified, safety measures will be established.
prevention and mitigation which will be aimed at:

3. DEFINITION OF ASSOCIATED BASIC CONCEPTS
For the purposes of this program it will be understood as:
a) Threat: Phenomenon of natural, biological or anthropic origin, which can cause
loss, damage or disruption to people, infrastructure, services, ways of life or
environment.
b) Threat of forest fire: Actions of people or existence of other factors
causes that will result in the probability of starting a forest fire.
c) Urban area: Corresponds to a group of more than 20 homes and where more than
100 inhabitants.
d) Public roads: Land communication routes intended for free transit, located
outside the urban limits of a population and whose strips are national assets for use
public.

e) Fuel: Corresponds to living and/or dead vegetation as a result of the natural cycle of the
vegetation or human interventions, which constitute material
available for the generation and propagation of forest fires.
f) Firebreak: Strip of land of determined width according to the height of the vegetation
surrounding area, which stops or hinders the spread of a forest fire due to the lack of
combustible vegetation or because it is not in a position to burn. Includes girdles
existing, such as roads, railway lines and waterways, and constructed firebreaks, such as
preventive firebreak, firebreak line, fire line, water firebreak and firebreak
chemist.
g) Potential damage: Quantitative or qualitative assessment that is assigned to an asset or resource to
protect. Its analysis can be carried out by integrating social, environmental and
economic, or only evaluating some of these aspects.
h) Waste (agricultural and forestry): Combustible plant material that remains as waste
of agricultural and forestry activities. For example: wheat stubble.
i) Fire: It is the result of the chemical process called combustion. For it to be
produce, there must be the presence of a fuel (for example, vegetation with low
moisture content), a heat source (campfire, matches, cigarettes, agricultural burning)
and oxygen (from the air).
j) Forest fire: Any destruction of vegetation, through fire and when
it spreads freely.
k) Fire of anthropic origin: Considered as an internal cause of fires, it is
generated either through carelessness, ignorance or intentionally.
l) Fire of natural origin: Considered as an external cause that, from its origin to a
fire, which are very rare in our country, such as eruptions
volcanics or lightning strikes in dry thunderstorms.
m) Fireproof: Non-combustible.

n) Mitigation of the risk of forest fire: Set of measures aimed at reducing to
maximize the potential effects on people, property and the environment in the

o) Prevention of the risk of forest fire: Set of measures designed to prevent
forest fires occur.
p) Danger: Corresponds to the environmental conditions that affect both the susceptibility
of vegetation to ignition and flammability (meteorology, topography), as well as to
the natural resistance present to control the spread of possible outbreaks of
fires that start in the area.

r) Risk of forest fires: It is the probability of exceeding a specific damage value
social, environmental and economic, in a given place and during a time of exposure
determined. The determination of the Risk of forest fires is given by the
following generic equation: Risk: (threat; vulnerability). Where the threat is
understood as the probability of occurrence of a forest fire, conditioned by
the presence and activity of a causal agent, vulnerability, for its part, corresponds
to the type of fuel possible to be affected.
s) Urban-rural interface zone: That transition zone where there is contact between
some plant formation with built, inhabited sectors and/or urban areas (Protocol
of Forest Plantations).
4. FOREST FIRES AND CONSEQUENCES
The problems caused by fire such as forest fire can be classified according to the damage and
effects. These are described in the following sections:
4.1 DAMAGES FROM FOREST FIRES
When we talk about damage, we are referring to the losses caused by a forest fire.
valued in money. Losses caused by forest fires, or improper burning
applied, they can be classified into two broad categories.

a) Direct damages: These are all damages caused to tangible assets, tradable in the
market in monetary values, whose identification and evaluation is feasible to carry out
immediately or within a short period of time after the action of the fire occurs. In this
category it is worth noting losses of wood and other forest products, crops
agricultural, fences, livestock, sawmills and other facilities.
b) Indirect damages: These correspond to losses of an intangible nature, difficult to
identify and evaluate, sometimes even in the medium term, with consequences that generally
They manifest themselves in values that were not directly affected by the action of the
fire. It is worth highlighting the loss of soil fertility, the initiation of processes
erosion, the silting of water courses, alterations in the biological balance and in
ecosystems, the deterioration of scenic beauty and environmental hygiene, and even the
sequel of economic and social problems that originate in communities whose
Livelihood is based mainly on the use of forest resources.
4.2 EFFECTS OF FOREST FIRES
It refers to the quality of the damage, or the alterations that originate in the dynamics of the
different processes related to renewable natural resources, including the functions that
These fulfill economic and social development.
The effects are aimed at establishing the losses or alterations caused by the fire
forestry. They are classified as socioeconomic and ecological.
a) Socioeconomic Effects
Public health:
• Pollution of soil, water and air.
• Deterioration of the landscape and scenic beauty.
• Limitations for recreation, leisure and sports practices.
• Vehicle accidents. The smoke generated by fire, through fires or burning
edges near roads, can cause traffic accidents, because the
Drivers' visibility is considerably reduced.
Community development:
• Property damage.
• Deterioration or stoppage of productive processes.

action of human origin, whether due to carelessness, ignorance or intentionally, that

be associated with natural causes, such as lightning, or due to unrelated causes or not caused by the
project.

6. CONTINGENCY PLAN
For the identified risks, in order to prevent forest fires,
will implement the following measures:
a) Electrical failure:
- The laying of cables, conductors and other electrical installations will be carried out by
properly trained personnel.
- Any modification and/or repair associated with electrical systems will be carried out by
qualified personnel.
- A prohibition will be established on installing exposed cables without impact protection.
- All electrical systems will be marked. In particular, the main boards and
devices in the electrical rooms will be marked, indicating the sectors to which
Each switch belongs.
- All necessary control measures will be established to avoid situations of
risk when work is carried out in the vicinity of energized power lines.
- Periodic reviews and maintenance will be carried out on all facilities that may be

attack on the fire source with the project's own resources, in order to carry out control in

total extinction in the event that control has not been achieved with the first attack.

Any contingency occurs within the scope of the project facilities. The structure
of the emergency brigade and its functions are described below:
- Emergency Director: Assumes maximum responsibility in the Brigade structure,

8.2 EMERGENCY LEVELS AND COMMUNICATIONS
Every time the emergency chief (or whoever replaces him) is notified about the existence of a
fire source, this must evaluate the magnitude or level of the fire and if it is possible to deal with it.

It should be noted that 3 emergency levels have been defined, based on these levels,
will determine the means of protection and combat, both human and material, that must be
be mobilized to deal with the fire. The defined emergency levels are:
- Level 1 or Emergency Attempt: Situation in which the risk or accident that causes it,
can be neutralized easily and quickly, with the available means and resources
present at the time and place of the accident.
- Level 2 or Partial Emergency: Situation in which the risk or accident requires to be
controlled the intervention of teams specifically designated and instructed for this purpose;
affects an area of the facility and may require “partial evacuation” or
evacuation of the affected area.
- Level 3 or General Emergency: Situation in which the risk or accident endangers the
security and physical integrity of people and it is necessary to proceed to the eviction or
evacuation, leaving the facilities. Requires the intervention of external help.
In the event that the fire has not been controlled with the project's own resources, the head
of the emergency must contact the emergency director and report the fact,
for the latter to make communications and request the support of the National Corporation
Forestry (CONAF) and Penco Fire Department.

9.1.1 About oxygen:
The burning fuel is covered with earth to isolate it from air and, therefore, oxygen.

9.1.2 About heat
The heat that allows combustion is absorbed by applying water to the fire. The water has the

It is consumed trying to evaporate the water. Evaporation, therefore, removes heat from the environment,

- Interrupting the horizontal and vertical continuity of the vegetation, cutting, extracting
and eliminating combustible vegetation in the path of the fire.
- Increasing the moisture content of the vegetation.
- Covering the fuel with a mixture of water with a chemical product that retards or

9.2 COMBAT METHODS
To control the fire, a direct attack will be carried out by installing the so-called “line
of fire” at the edge of the fire source, applying water, throwing earth, by suffocation,
pushing burning material in, cutting vegetation, clearing and scraping the
forest or scrub floor to mineral soil. With this method it is cut immediately
the spread of fire, because control is on the front of the advance itself.
9.2.1 Line of Fire
It consists of distributing the members of each group forming a line, maintaining
permanently its same relative position and advancing at the same speed. The displacement
of men is carried out in such a way that each of them performs a part of the work,
achieving that the line is completely made with the pass of the last combatant.
