1 INTRODUCTION
This report provides the results of the baseline characterization of fauna (wild animals)
associated with the “Clay Extraction Project for the Production of Rare Earth Concentrate” (hereinafter “the
Project"), which will be located in the communes of Penco (mostly) and Concepción, province of
Concepción, Biobío region, and its sensitivity in relation to the planned activities and works. It should be noted
that, for the preparation of this report, the guidelines established in the “Guide for the
Description of the area of influence: Description of the Soil, Flora and Fauna Components of Terrestrial Ecosystems
in the SEIA (SEA 2015), “Guide on the Area of Influence in the Environmental Impact Assessment System” (SEA
2017) and the Guide “Evaluation Criteria in the SEIA: Technical Criteria for Terrestrial Fauna Land Campaigns
and Data Validation” (SEA 2022).
In this way, the characterization of the component was carried out based on the requirements of the Safety System.
Environmental Impact Assessment, within the framework of what is established by Law No. 19,300 on General Environmental Bases
Environment and its subsequent modifications, and specifically, letter e.2) of Article 18 of the D.S. No. 40 of 2012
Ministry of the Environment, which modified the Regulation of the Environmental Impact Assessment System (RSEIA).
2 OBJECTIVES
2.1 GENERAL OBJECTIVE
The objective of this report is to ecologically characterize the community of mesomammals present in the Area
of influence of the Project, presenting aspects that could be relevant in the context of the execution of works
of this.
2.2 SPECIFIC OBJECTIVES
• Identify the environments present in the area of influence of the Project.
• Evaluate the spatial and temporal patterns of mesomammal species in the Area of influence
of the project
• Relate the patterns of occurrence with the environments present in the Project Area of influence.
• Estimate the abundance of each mesomammal species in the Project Area of influence.
• Identify sites of importance for the group of species within the Project Area of Influence

3 AREA OF INFLUENCE
3.1 DETERMINATION OF THE AREA OF INFLUENCE
The area of influence is located in the cities of Penco and Concepción, homonymous communes, in the province of
Concepción, Biobío Region. The area where the Project will be located corresponds to a sector east of Route 150
and crosses Route 152 through the center of the area, with an extension of 3.0 km by 2.5 km and an intervention area of 169.03
ha. This area includes a buffer area considering a width of approximately 200 m from the limit of the areas.
of the project towards the outside, forming a polygon with a total area of 673.62 hectares (Figure 3-1). This
buffer area, is greater than the Project area
, since it considers an extra surface that could potentially be
affected by the development of the Project activities, considering the inherent displacement capacity
that has the fauna component.
3.1.1 Justification of the Area of Influence
The extension of the area of influence of the Project is directly determined by each environmental component,
their interrelationships or natural dynamics and the nature of the action or effect induced by the Project. Considering
The above, the dimension of the area of influence has a geographical and territorial extension in relation to the variable
terrestrial vertebrate fauna, therefore, this area includes the geographical variation for each taxon, covering aspects
of the biology of each group and the interactions with the abiotic environment.
For the definition and description of the area of influence of the terrestrial vertebrate fauna component, the following steps were followed:
guidelines of the “Guide for the description of soil, flora and fauna components of terrestrial ecosystems in the
SEIA” (SEA 2015) and the “Guide for the description of the area of influence in the SEIA” (SEA 2017), which consider the
following aspects: preliminary identification of possible effects, spatial extension of the parts, works and/or
Project activities, the geographical space of the impact-receiving elements, relevant attributes and
current situation. Once these aspects were defined, the area of influence where they can manifest was established.
the effects on the fauna component resulting from the parts and works of the Project, as well as the development of its
activities (Figure 3-1).

4.2 SAMPLING DESIGN
4.2.1 Sampling points
In order to obtain photographic records of meso-mammals present in the area of influence of the project,
The olfactory station method was used in combination with the use of camera traps (O'Connell et al.
2011), which were distributed in the AI as shown in Figure 4-2. This methodology consists of
installation of motion-sensitive cameras of the Bushnell Trophy Cam type, along with the use of a bait (attractor),
in places close to possible transit sites for species of the target group, such as trails or bodies of water,
thus managing to capture more elusive or less frequent species photographically.
Subsequently, the memory card of each camera is removed and the photos are analyzed in the cabinet to
recognize the registered species and confirm their presence in the AI. The effort dedicated to this methodology is
based, according to the criteria of the specialists, on the type of terrain or environments in which the sampling point was located,
that is, the suitability of the sector to record presences of large mammals, especially
carnivores.
For the recognition of the species, the descriptions proposed by Acosta & Simonetti (1999) were considered.
Skewes (2009), Muñoz-Pedreros & Yáñez (2009) and Iriarte (2008) and for identification at the individual level,
morphometric characteristics, sex, behavior and age, as well as traceable singularities of each of the
registered individuals.
Each of the 26 installed cameras was georeferenced and programmed to record photographs. The Chambers
Traps were installed in July 2022 and were monitored with semiannual campaigns until July 2023.
The design sought to better encompass the AI and the different fauna environments identified. Location details
of each of the chambers is presented in Table 4-3.

4.2.3 Spatial and temporal patterns of mesomammals, and their relationship with the environments present in the
project area
4.2.3.1 Spatial patterns
For this objective, two simple population parameters were calculated and widely used for the study of
mammal species: i) the simple (or naïve) occupancy parameter was calculated. Occupation is defined as
proportion of areas, patches or places occupied by a species (MacKenzie et. al. 2006). The estimate of the
Simple occupancy takes into account the presence and absence of the species, while real occupancy takes into account the
“present but not detected” state variable (i.e., probability of imperfect detection or p<1); ii) the rate
detection rate (RAI) expressed as the number of independent records per 100 trap-days (# reg/100
trap-day). This index that provides information on the patterns of occurrence/activity of a species in a
certain area from sampling stations deployed over a period of time (Rovero & Marshall
2009). To do this, the photographs of each species of mesomammals in each camera trap were processed, and
calculated the number of independent recordings (those separated at least every 60 min) using the Camera program
Sweet (https://smallcats.org/resources/). With the number of records and the sampling effort of each camera
trap, the detection rates (RAI) of each species of mesomammal at each station were calculated.
sampling (camera trap). The RAI index was compared between the different environments to evaluate the importance of
these as habitat for each species. Furthermore, on a finer scale, the RAI of each species was compared among the
different stations deployed in the study area in order to identify those sites with the greatest activity
of each species of mesomammal. For this last analysis, the kernel density function was used to
Explicitly spatialize the results by distributing them in deciles.

The set of photographic records of each species was used to evaluate the temporal activity patterns of each

developed by Ridou and Linkie (2009) and implemented in the R program, the activity curve of the
species in the study area. The activity graph was centered at midnight, assuming that the species
They could have a predominantly nocturnal behavior.

4.2.3.3 Identification of mesocarnivores and comparison between sites
The systematized records obtained for each species in each camera trap through the use of the program
Camera Sweet (see previous section) were distributed among four mesomammal specialists in order to
perform the identification of individuals for each species. To do this, individuals were identified by obvious marks
(twisted tails, scars and cuts on the ears), less obvious marks (scars), discoloration of the tip of the
tail and rings) and by subtle markings (spots, patterns, coloration on the underside of the legs, carriage of the tail and
shape and carriage of the body (Fig. 2). This type of approach has previously been used to identify individuals
in species that present an important variation in their morphological pattern such as jaguars, tigers and ocelots
(Karanth et al. 1995, Silver et al. 2004, Di Bitetti et al. 2006), as well as low morphological variation such as
pumas (Kelly et al. 2018). Each researcher classified each individual's records into positive identifications,
provisional and indistinguishable identifications (usually an unusable photo due to poor
quality).
4.2.3.4 Density and movement of mesomammals
The number of individuals identified per species was used to estimate the population density of each one. For
determine the size of the sampled area, the minimum and maximum distance traveled by the species was calculated
(considered as movement) between cameras (DMRC), and half of each distance (1/2 DMRC) was used as the radius
buffer around each camera trap location (Long et al. 2008). In parallel, a
histogram of distances by species to explore the maximum and minimum dispersal ranges recorded. The areas
Overlapping buffers were dissolved and total area was calculated in Arc View (ESRI 1999) using X-tools or Spatial.
Analyst. Then, the number of individuals of each species was divided by the total study area to obtain the
density of each species. This estimation method is one of the simplest because it does not consider the history
capture-recapture of individuals as has been addressed in other studies (Kelly et al. 2018).

shown in Table 5-3 and Figure 5-1. When analyzing the detection rates by environment, it is observed that the rate of

with respect to the other three environments (Figure 5-2).

6 DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
The monitoring carried out between January and July 2023 along 26 stations with camera traps (1,288 traps-
night), detected the presence of four species of meso-mammals within the area of influence of the Project,
all of them of native origin and listed in some conservation category. A total of 736 records were obtained
independent of the target group, showing differences in the occurrence of these in the four environments
identified in the area of influence. The species with the highest number of records is Lycalopex culpaeus (fox
culpeo), with 662 records (90%), followed by Leopardus guigna (güiña) with 38 records (5%). Galictis species
cuja (quique) and Pudu puda (pudú) obtained 21 (3%) and 15 (2%) registrations respectively. When analyzing the rates of
detections of each of these species using the RAI index, the greater activity of the culpeo fox stands out in
Plantation, with respect to the guiña, pudú and quique whose detections are greater in native forest (in the case of the pudú
is slightly higher). A similar pattern is observed when analyzing the proportion of sites where each of the species
was detected, that is, the occupation. On the other hand, the environment with the highest occurrence of records was “Plantation”,
accumulating 64% of the total registrations, followed by “Bosque” with 32%. The “Modified” environments and
“Matorral” accumulate 3% and 1% respectively. This coincides with the largest area of forest plantations
inside the study area (54%), followed by the native forest (21.98%) mainly in streams. The
Use patterns found in this monitoring are consistent with what was reported by Moreira-Arce et al. (2015) for
meso carnivores of the temperate coastal forest, where the culpeo fox and the guiña are mostly detected species
(83% and 75%, respectively). The dominance of the surface of plantations and native forest in the study area
It is also a factor that could explain the greater presence of records in both environments. However, it is
known the ecological generalissimo of the culpeo fox and the guiña, which may be factors that explain the presence
of these species in different inland environments, and particularly in forest plantations. These last ones,
if found within native understory, they can also act as complementary habitat for species
present providing native and exotic prey (Moreira-Arce et al. 2015 and 2016). In the case of pudú, whose
detection was slightly higher in native forest compared to plantation (it was only recorded in these environments),
This is because this environment offers better conditions in terms of shelter and more nutritionally rich food, which
which leads species like Pudu puda to prefer them over other environments (Pavez-Fox & Estay 2016).
The spatial pattern obtained through kernel analyzes shows variations for each of the species
evaluated, showing higher detection rates in the central-southern zone of the project's area of influence for the
species Lycalopex culpaeus and Leopardus guigna, while for Pudu puda and Galictis cuja this occurs towards areas
more located and
n the eastern and southwestern edges of the area of ​​influence. The area of the study area where the rate of
detection is greater for the entire target group is the area located in the center-southern of the area of influence,
corresponding mostly to “Plantation” and “Forest” environments, which is surrounded by roads and
It limits to the north with a storage area and to the south with an extraction area according to the project works plan. A
higher degree of occupancy by the species evaluated in this area, suggests the importance that this site
could have for the mesomammal group, probably due to a greater availability of resources such as
food, water or shelter.
Regarding the temporal patterns of activity of the identified mesomammals, or also known as
circadian rhythms, the culpeo and guiña fox species show a homogeneous distribution in terms of hours
of activity, with a slight peak in the case of the latter at times close to midnight and before dawn.
The pudú shows markedly nocturnal behavior, in contrast to the quique which shows patterns of
daytime activity. Spatial patterns reported by Zúñiga et al. (2016) in Temperate Forests
coastal regions of the IX region show a more marked pattern of these species: rather diurnal and nocturnal of the fox
culpeo and wink, respectively. On the other hand, the results of this monitoring on the nocturnal patterns of the

pudú if they are consistent with what was reported for the species by Zúñiga et al. (2018) for the coastal temperate forest
of the VIII and IX Region. The circadian ritual is a trait usually considered adaptive and determined mainly
by physiological characteristics (Kronfeld-Schor & Dayan 2003) and by evolutionary history (Halle 2000; Roll et
al. 2006). However, the circadian rhythm is not only defined by endogenous characteristics of a species, but
which is also modulated by multiple environmental factors, such as seasonal variation (Caravaggi et al. 2018),
food availability (Pereira 2010), predation (Caravaggi et al. 2018), competition (Monterroso et
al. 2014; Bhatt et al. 2021) or anthropogenic disturbances (Zaman et al. 2022), factors that may be varying
between the Project area and the less anthropized areas where the studies by Zúñiga et al. were carried out.
Based on the detections of the species and the identifications carried out by four specialists, it was recorded
a total of 44 individuals, the most abundant species being the culpeo fox. Although this identification exercise
involved specialists in carnivorous mammals, the variation in the number of individuals identified was high
particularly for the culpeo fox and the quique, species that do not present morphological differences. In the case of
the guiña, its pattern of spots, and in the case of the pudú, the presence of antlers in the males allowed to have
a lower rate of variation between specialists. The abundance of individuals per chamber was not distributed
homogeneously between the stations, highlighting the CT15 and CTpudu01 chambers with the greatest number of individuals
of fox blame. Something similar was found in the case of the quique whose abundance was much higher in the chamber
CT pudu01.
According to the records in the different stations, the dispersion of each species evaluated was calculated, showing values
similar dispersal among species, despite differing in their body sizes. The species that recorded the smallest
Dispersion value was the quique, while the culpeo fox recorded the highest value. 2 scenarios were calculated,
where the greatest dispersion has a total area of 16.61 km2, while the minimum scenario has a total of 7.78 km2.
The density of individuals was different in both scenarios, with values in the minimum dispersion scenario of 0.77.
ind/km2 for pudú, up to 2.96 ind/km2 in the case of the culpeo fox. The same occurs in the maximum scenario
dispersion, where the values for these species range from 0.36 ind/km2, to 1.38 ind/km2 for pudu and fox
I blame respectively. est
The values are consistent with what is reported in the literature for some of these
species. For example, the culpeo fox has the most information about distribution and home ranges. The
Density values reported in the literature range from 0.2 – 2.6 ind/km2 (Gurtiñas et al 2021), while the
Home ranges range from 6 – 9 km2 in females and 2-10 km2 for males. In the case of the wink, the values
Reported for this species in fragmented landscapes vary between 0.05 to 0.77 ind /km2. The rest of the species of
This study has general information about its distribution based on specific studies in certain areas of
interest such as protected areas in the case of Pudu puda, so the information obtained here in this
The study is not only relevant to the scope of the Project, but also contributes to the knowledge of the history
nature of these species and thus improve their conservation plans in the central-southern area of Chile.
