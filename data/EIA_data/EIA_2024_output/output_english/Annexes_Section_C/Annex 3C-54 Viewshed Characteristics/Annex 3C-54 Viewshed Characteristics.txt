1 CHARACTERIZATION OF VIEW WATERSHEDS
As noted in the Landscape Value Baseline, the viewshed (CV) corresponds to the portion of
terrain visible from a given PO. In geographical terms it represents all those elements
that the observer can perceive in its real location (Español, 1998).
Operationally, the CVs are obtained through modeling in ArcGIS 10.5®, in the module
Viewshed, which automatically delivers the viewshed from a point or route, based on the
topographic characteristics obtained through a Digital Terrain Model or DEM. However, the
Theoretical viewsheds are adjusted based on the line-of-sight condition, which corresponds to the
direct observation of the field specialist, identifying visual barriers in situ, such as topography
mainly given by the high peaks that frame the visual basins, the vegetation or the
anthropic structures. The delimitation and analysis of the VC consider maximum thresholds of vision or
critical visibility distances, since as objects move away from the observer, their details change
ceasing to be perceived, until a moment comes when the complete object ceases to be distinguished.
Taking into account what is stated in the Guide for the Environmental Impact Assessment of Landscape Value
In the SEIA of the SEA (2019), for this Baseline it is considered the maximum or critical distance of
visual range 3,500 m, since from that distance the observer stops perceiving clearly a
given object.
A
Once the CVs are defined, they are characterized based on the following parameters:
• Size: is the amount of surface seen from the observation point. It can be big or
large, medium and small.
• Shape: relates to the geometric shape of the basin. It can be circular, semicircular, elongated
and irregular.
• View types: corresponds to the views obtained from the basin. They can be panoramic (limits
distant and generalized view of place); focused (very closed basins where the eye focuses
directly to a specific point); closed (foregrounds and backgrounds are seen); and mixed.
• Compactness: corresponds to the greater or lesser presence of unseen areas (shadow) within the
viewshed, that is, it refers to the number of hidden areas within a given
view.
• Position of the observer: corresponds to the relationship between the location of the observer and the position
spatial of the elements of the landscape, determined mainly by the topography, which
conditions the appreciation of the scenic composition of the ensemble. According to this, the
observer can be located on the plain, at the bottom of the valley, mid-slope or on the upper edge
of the slope (or top).

associated with routes and access roads close to the Project installation area.
